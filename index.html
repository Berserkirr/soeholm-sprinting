Reaction Time Correction — The Core Fix in This Version
Omega Timing FAT system documentation · IAAF Technical Rules (TR26.3)
All FAT competition times include reaction time from gun-to-first-movement. The competition split PDFs (Henson, 2023) record reaction times individually — mean was 0.149s (range 0.090–0.246s). Practice times do not include reaction time because athletes self-initiate or react to a "go" call without a timing gun. The correct conversion is: pure_movement_time = FAT_split − reaction_time. We then apply only the mechanical advantage of blocks (not RT) when converting to 3-point practice: +0.42s at 30m, +0.50s at 50m, +0.58s at 60m. This total offset accounts for (1) no block projection force, (2) practice conditions vs competition freshness, and (3) self-timing vs gate timing.
SOURCE: Omega Timing (2023). Diamond League Race Analysis documentation. Henson T (2023). Men's 100m Split Times by Meeting — 400 records with paired reaction times parsed from 143-page PDF.
Practice Fly Times vs Race Fly Times (+0.01 to +0.03s slower in practice)
User observation confirmed by elite coaching literature · Colyer et al. (2017), J Sports Sci
Flying sprint times measured in practice (with 30m rolling build-up, laser gates) are consistently 0.01–0.03s slower than the equivalent velocity reached in competition. This is due to: (1) competition arousal raising peak neuromuscular output by ~1–3%; (2) taper/freshness in competition vs cumulative training load; (3) race stimulus from competitors. In this tool a midpoint offset of +0.02s is added to race-derived fly benchmarks to produce practice targets. The race Vmax itself is measured from the 60–80m split interval (reaction time cancels since it shifts both marks equally), then practice fly = 10/Vmax + 0.02 (per fly distance, not scaled).
SOURCE: Colyer SL, Nagahara R, Salo AI. (2017). Kinetic demands of sprinting shift across the acceleration phase: Novel analysis of transient kinetics. Scand J Med Sci Sports. DOI: 10.1111/sms.12869
60m as Strongest Single Practice Predictor (R² = 0.92)
Real-data regression from this dataset: t100 = 1.744 × t60_block − 1.286
The 60m split (block start, FAT) has the highest R² among single-distance predictors of 100m time in this dataset. It captures both the acceleration phase AND the onset of maximum velocity, which together account for ~65% of the race. For practice, the 3-point conversion adds +0.58s to the pure competition 60m time to yield the expected practice benchmark. A 10.0s athlete's pure 60m is ~6.33s; their practice 3pt 60m target is ~6.91s.
SOURCE: Regression computed from 400 RT-corrected FAT competition records, Sep 2023 Henson dataset. R²=0.916 for FAT 60m → 100m finish time.
Maximum Velocity — Near-Perfect Predictor (r ≈ −0.96)
Haugen et al. (2019) PMC6872694 · Macadam et al. (2022) Eur J Sport Sci
Vmax (measured at the 60–80m segment in competition) has near-perfect negative correlation with 100m time. In this dataset, Vmax ranges from 12.03 m/s (9.7s athletes) to 9.72 m/s (11.5s athletes). These values are reaction-time-independent — the 60–80m interval subtracts the same RT from both marks. A 10.0s athlete has average Vmax of 11.55 m/s; their practice fly20 (30m build-up) target is 1.751s = 20/11.55 + 0.02.
SOURCE: Haugen T, Seiler S, Sandbakk Ø, Tønnessen E. (2019). The Training and Development of Elite Sprint Performance. Sports Med Open 5:44. PMC6872694.
Speed Endurance: 120m & 150m @ 95% Effort
Schexnayder B (Complete Track and Field) · Pfaff D (USATF Level 2 Sprint Coaching)
At 95% effort, velocity-specific adaptations still occur (minimum threshold per Haugen 2019). The 120m@95% models the full 100m race demand plus 20m of maintained speed. Conversion: t120_max = race_t100 + 20/(Vmax × 0.835). Then t120_95 = t120_max ÷ 0.955. The 150m@95% is Schexnayder's primary 200m readiness test — athletes should reach within ~0.5s of this benchmark to be considered race-ready. For 200m-specific training, the 150m@95% is calibrated to 200m race pace (≈ t200 × 0.762 ÷ 0.955), not 100m pace.
SOURCE: Schexnayder B. Complete Track and Field. Human Kinetics (2014). Pfaff D. USATF Level 2 Speed/Power Event Coaching Manual. Sprint physiology conversion model validated against competition data.
200m Pacing: First-Half Split Data from 1,383 Real Races
Henson T (2025). Men's 200m Split Times by Time (207 pages, Omega/Seiko FAT data)
From 1,383 200m races with FAT split data, the first-100m split averages 10.14s for 19.4s athletes to 11.57s for 22.8s athletes. These first splits are intentionally paced ~0.10–0.15s slower than standalone 100m capability. The 200m-to-first-split ratio ranges from 1.921 (elite) to 1.975 (developing), confirming that elite 200m runners have superior speed reserve and second-half maintenance. All 200m practice benchmarks in this tool are derived from the corresponding 100m speed capacity implied by these first-half splits, then adjusted for 200m-specific speed endurance.
SOURCE: Henson T (2025). Men's 200m Split Times by Time. Personal compilation. 1,383 validated records, range 19.19–22.85s. Race analyses from Omega Timing, Seiko, KSSB, Graubner (2008–2025).
